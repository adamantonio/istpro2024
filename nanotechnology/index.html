Nanotechnology

Explore the microscopic realm through nanotechnology!

What is nanotechnology?

According to the National Nanotechnology Initiative (NNI) “nanotechnology is the understanding and control of matter at the nanoscale, at dimensions between approximately 1 and 100 nanometres”. It is said that biological properties of matter can be displayed at a nanoscale, which is different from properties of single atoms, molecules, and bulk materials.

Fun fact 1: Nanotechnology has the ability to provide a cancer cure without side effects

Compared to other forms or sizes of the same materials, nanostructured materials are often stronger or have different magnetic properties while others are better at conducting heat or electricity. Nanomaterials can also become more reflective of light, chemically reactive or change colours as their structure and/or size is altered.

The purpose of nanotechnology

The purpose of nanotechnology is to help improve, and revolutionise technology and industry sectors. These sectors include information technology, homeland security, medicine, transportation, food safety, environment science, and many more.

Fun fact 2: A sphere that’s 1 nanometre in diameter is like comparing the size of a softball to the size of earth.

How small is nano?

The prefix “nano” means one billionth or 10-9 in the International System of Units, which is one billionth of a meter. The unit for nanometer is nm. To make is easier to visualise it, a sheet of paper is 100,000 nanometres thick, and a human hair is around 50,000 to 100,000 nanometres in diameter.

Products made using Nanotechnology

Sunscreen

Over the years, nanoparticles have been added to sunscreen to make them more effective. Two of the most commonly added nanoparticles are titanium dioxide and zinc oxide. These tiny particles make a great addition to sunscreen as it is not only highly effective at blocking UV radiation, but it also feels lighter on the skin. This is why present-day sunscreen is nowhere as thick and tacky as it was in the past.

Clothing

Fabrics that repel water and other liquids can be created with the help nanoparticles of silica. In textiles, the silica that is added to the fabric is either sprayed onto the surface or incorporated into the fabric’s weave to create a stainproof or waterproof coating. Thanks to nanotechnology, when liquid comes in contact with waterproof clothing, it simply forms little water droplets that roll off rather than being absorbed.

Fun fact 3: scientists are trying to use nanotechnology to creat an invisibility cloak.

Adhesives

Adhesives can also be optimised through the use of nanotechnology. At high temperatures, most glues lose their stickiness. To prevent this, a powerful “nano-glue” can not only withstand high temperatures, but it also gets stronger as surrounding temperatures increase.

Types of Nanomaterials

Natural nanomaterials naturally occur in the world, which include the particles that make up smoke or volcanic ash. These can also include some molecules in our bodies, for example the haemoglobin in our blood. Another example is a peacock’s feathers, which are a result of spacing between nanometer-scale structures on the surface.

Artificial nanomaterials, however, occur from things or processes created by people. It can be divided into four categories:

  1. Carbon-based
  2. Metal-based
  3. Dendrimers
  4. Composites

Some examples include the exhaust from fossil fuel when burning engines, which forms pollution.

Tools used for Nanotechnology

Microscopes

Microscopes, specifically an atomic force microscope (AFM), is very important in nanotechnology as it is used to detect the particle size and chemical composition of nanoparticles. Not only does the microscope do this, it is also useful in processing the nanoparticles.


Fun fact 5: Your fingernails grow around 1 nanometer per second

Probes

Probes, specifically imaging probes or biomarkers are mostly used in the medical sector of nanotechnology. In this sector, probes are used to track specific molecular pathways in a living system, to detect diseases, as well as imaging and tracking single cells, tumors, etc. This can be especially useful as it can detect diseases early through molecular imaging, allowing treatment to be provided.

Lithography Systems

Lithography systems are involved in the creation of intricate patterns on an underlying substance or layer, which is often a thin film of silicon or other types of semiconducting materials.

Software Used in the Modelling of Nanostructures

NanoLanguage


Fun fact 6: Through nanotechnology, self cleaning surfaces are made possible

NanoLanguage is a scripting interface which is built on top of Python. Its main use is to simulate and visualise the properties of nanoscale systems, both physical and chemical.

Atomistix ToolKit (ATK)

Atomistix ToolKit is quite similar to NanoLanguage. It is a commercial software which allows atomic-scale modelling and simulation of nano systems. This software is actually the numerical engine that carries out the simulations from Virtual NanoLab.

Atomistix Virtual NanoLab (VNL)

The Atomistix Virtual NanoLab is a commercial point-and-click software which is used to simulate and analyse physical and chemical properties of nanoscale devices. It contains a graphical interface which provides user-friendly approaches to modelling at an atomic scale.

In the software, there is a set of interactive instruments which allow users to design nano systems, setup numerical calculations and to be able to visualise the results. With just a few mouse clicks, samples of molecules, nanotubes, crystalline systems and two-probe systems can be built.

Virtual NanoLab also contains a 3D visualisation tool known as the Nanoscope. The Nanoscope allows users to view and analyse atomic geometries and computed results.

Effects of Nanotechnology on Society

Positive Effects

Renewable Energy

New ways of obtaining and storing energy are enabled through nanotechnology. Solar panels are also made cheaper and more effective. Nanotechnology will bring many positive effects to energy as it lowers costs, produces stronger and lighter wind turbines, improves fuel efficiency and can save energy through the thermal insulation of certain nanocomponents.

Electronics

Nanotechnology makes it possible to build very precise circuits at an atomic level as silicon microchips are replaced with nanochips. Moreover, carbon nanotubes have the potential to replace silicon as it will make smaller, faster and more efficient microchips and devices. Carbon nanotubes are also lighter and conductive.

Fun fact 7: When nanomaterials were injected into mice, they were able to regain the ability to use paralysed limbs.

Medicine

Through nanotechnology, medicine can be made more effective as it allows arteries to be unblocked, cells to be selectively attacked, damaged genes to be repaired and more precise and faster surgeries can be performed. Some nanomaterials can also be used to improve early diagnosis and treatment for diseases and cancer as they are able to selectively attack cancer cells without harming other healthy cells.

Negative Effects

The Environment

Nanotechnology threatens the environment as new toxins and pollutants are generated and released into the atmosphere.

Job Market

Nanotechnology may have a negative impact on the Job Market as outdated materials and change sin production processes has the potential to destroy jobs. However, this new technology also has the ability to create other jobs, however this will be later in the future.

Safety

Safety could be compromised as properties of nanotechnology could lead to the production of nano weapons and smart bullets, possibly facilitating espionage.

Issues with Nanotechnology

Legal Issues

Regulations

Fun fact 8: The difference between 1nm and 1cm is like comparing a golf ball to earth

Nanotechnology is a very vast field that includes a wide range of technologies at a nano scale. Some fields include pharmaceuticals, biotechnology, genomics, neuroscience, robotics, etc. Since nanotechnology is still very new, there are global debates about risk regulation and international cooperation.

The potential risks with nanoparticles and nanotechnology have been acknowledged by regulatory bodies around the world. Starting from 2004, the European Union are in the process of developing a regulatory policy which will tighten control. This will also improve knowledge about the risks of nanotechnology. As of today, provisions have been introduced on nanomaterials. These were mainly introduced for biocides, cosmetics, food additives, food labelling, etc.

Ethical Issues

Health concerns

Nanoparticles are danger for many reasons. One of which is that nanoparticles may damage a person’s lungs when breathed in. This is because of the size of these particles and also because of the chemically they carry, such as metals or hydrocarbons. Studies have shown that some nanoparticles can cause lung damage in rats. While other studies show that nanoparticles can lead to brain damage in fish and dogs. Learn more from The European Commision.

Uncertainties with nanoparticles

A big issue is that it is nearly impossible to know if a product or substance contains nanoparticles because these are not required to be labelled under the current legislation. Due to their small size, nanomaterials interact with humans in different ways than normal materials.

Recommendations

Improving knowledge base

Fun fact 9: The oldest form of nanotechnology can be dated back to fourth century AD by the Romans

Nanotechnology is a rapidly growing industry that include many people working to advance the science and to develop the applications of it. However, resource or information gaps need to be a subject of focus, especially for funding agencies.

Understanding of Risks

A majority of the public and private investment in research and development (R&D) funds should go towards a better understanding of the risks, especially in the production, application exposure or disposal of nanomaterials and products. This involves characterising the risks, while also assessing and managing them. The knowledge gaps that exist today need to be closed and fast and responsive warning and monitoring systems should also be developed.

Broadening Social Benefits and Acceptance

As society learns more and more about nanotechnology and the potential risks associated with it, discussions about exploring the social and moral implications of future innovations should be broadened. There are applications that has the ability to offer universal benefits, such as life-saving medical treatments, however these may be inaccessible due to the cost, especially for those struggling financially. Solutions to social challenges such as water treatments or energy generation can potentially be solved through nanotechnology. However, if these applications are not widely shared across the globe, the split between developed and developing nations will only grow larger.

The public’s interest

The investment in research and development fund should be aimed at wider social applications, especially towards the benefit of the public.

Developing countries

For nanotechnology’s benefits to be shared globally, new approaches to intellectual property rights need to be considered. To many developing countries, applications of nanotechnology with have significant benefits, such as the need for clean drinking water. Through this, access to nanotechnology applications will be made more affordable.
